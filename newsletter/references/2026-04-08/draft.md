# Art That Moves the World

The Rothko painting at the SFMOMA is identifiable to anyone who's ever seen it. Formally titled *No. 14* (1960), it is a nine-foot field of deep red and blue that claims all the space in the room. My first experience with it was defensive and confused — not poetic enough to write a poem about. So instead, I wrote a bad script:

The Rothko calls me into the room.
I scoff a little but move towards it.
The Rothko says nothing.
I pace back and forth, side to side, wiggling.
The Rothko says nothing.
There is a bench so I sit down at my designated distance.
The Rothko says nothing.
Painful minutes pass by, my palms are sweaty.
The Rothko says nothing.
I leave the room.

I had seen *No. 14* a hundred times in photographs. None of them prepared me. I had always considered Rothko's color blocks audacious, overdone, even tacky. Standing in front of the real thing undid that prejudice entirely. The painting was more polite than I expected. It didn't demand anything. It just held the room and waited.

Rothko once said, "Silence is so accurate." He wanted viewers to stand eighteen inches from the canvas — close enough that the color field fills the entire visual field, like being inside it. He insisted his paintings were not about color at all: "I'm interested only in expressing basic human emotions — tragedy, ecstasy, doom." And yet most people walk past *No. 14* in seconds. Some take a photo. A few sit down and leave when nothing happens.

The discomfort I felt in front of that painting had nothing to do with Rothko. It had everything to do with me. I didn't know how to sit with something that refused to explain itself. I wanted a caption, an audio guide, a wall text that would tell me what to feel. Rothko doesn't offer that. He asks you to stay.

---

I think about this discomfort often because it extends far beyond the museum. We live inside a flood of images — advertisements, feeds, interfaces, architecture — and we process almost none of them with any real attention. We scroll past more visual information in a single morning than most people encountered in a lifetime two centuries ago. And yet our capacity to interpret what we see has not kept pace. We consume images constantly and understand them less and less.

John Berger opens *Ways of Seeing* (1972) with a line that has stayed with me: "Seeing comes before words. The child looks and recognizes before it can speak." Seeing is our first language. It precedes literacy, precedes speech, precedes thought as we know it. And yet we treat visual language as ornamental — something reserved for artists and designers, not for citizens. We don't teach people how to read an image the way we teach them how to read a sentence.

This matters because visual language is inherently political. The way we react to a campaign poster, a skyline, a corporate logo, a protest photograph — all of it is shaped by how we've trained ourselves to see. Or, more often, by how we haven't. Sontag writes in *On Photography* (1977) that photographs "make normative an experience of art that is mediated, second-hand, intense in a different way." The image replaces the encounter. The photograph of the Rothko replaces the room. And once experience is mediated at that scale, the distance between seeing and understanding becomes almost unbridgeable.

The frustration people feel in front of abstract art is a symptom of this larger condition. We want the right answer. We want to know what the painting means, what the artist intended, whether our interpretation is correct. Abstract art doesn't give us that. It asks us to sit in ambiguity, to form an original thought without a prompt, to tolerate the discomfort of not knowing. Most of us lack the patience. We have been trained by algorithms and multiple-choice tests to expect resolution, and when a painting offers none, we assume the failure belongs to the art.

It doesn't. The failure belongs to us.

---

And yet, there are places in the world where art has done something that policy, economics, and technology could not. Places where the imagination of artists reshaped how an entire city thinks about itself.

In 1997, the Guggenheim Museum Bilbao opened in a declining industrial city in the Basque Country. Frank Gehry's titanium-clad building was, by any measure, an act of architectural imagination so audacious it bordered on absurd. Bilbao was not Paris or New York. It was a rusting port city hemorrhaging jobs and population. The idea that a single museum could reverse that trajectory seemed naive. But it did. Within a year, the museum had drawn nearly 1.4 million visitors. Hotels, restaurants, and cultural institutions followed. Economists now call it the "Bilbao Effect" — the demonstration that a single building, born from artistic vision, could become infrastructure. The imagination of one architect became the economic engine of an entire region.

In Venezuela, the musician and economist José Antonio Abreu founded El Sistema in 1975 with a different conviction: that music education was not a luxury for the privileged but a right for every child. He placed instruments in the hands of children living in poverty — not as charity, but as architecture. Social architecture. El Sistema grew into a national network of over four hundred youth orchestras, reaching hundreds of thousands of children across the country. It produced Gustavo Dudamel, now the conductor of the New York Philharmonic. More importantly, it produced generations of young people who learned discipline, collaboration, and expression through the daily practice of making music together. Abreu understood that art doesn't merely reflect a society — it builds one.

Perhaps the most striking example is Bogotá under Antanas Mockus. A mathematician and philosopher who became mayor in 1995, Mockus governed through creative intervention rather than enforcement. He replaced traffic cops with mimes who shamed jaywalkers. He distributed 350,000 cards printed with a thumbs-up on one side and a thumbs-down on the other, asking citizens to hold each other accountable. He painted stars on the pavement where pedestrians had been killed. Traffic fatalities dropped by more than fifty percent. Homicides fell by seventy percent. Mockus understood something that most politicians never grasp: changing how people see themselves in a city changes how they behave. The intervention was not legislative. It was imaginative. It was, in the deepest sense, artistic.

---

These are not stories about art as decoration or cultural enrichment. They are stories about art as a civic force — the force of imagination applied to the conditions of real life.

Doris Sommer, a professor at Harvard and author of *The Cultural Agent* (2014), argues that artists and humanists are not peripheral to civic life but essential to it. She calls them "cultural agents" — people who use creative practice to intervene in social and political reality. The artist's power, in Sommer's framework, is not the power to make beautiful objects. It is the power to imagine alternatives. To see what does not yet exist and propose it as though it could.

This is the skill we are losing. Not artistic skill — imaginative skill. The patience to sit with an open question long enough to arrive at an original thought. The willingness to tolerate ambiguity, to resist the compulsion for immediate resolution, to hold two contradictory ideas in mind without collapsing into certainty. These are the capacities that abstract art demands of its viewers. They are also the capacities that democratic life demands of its citizens.

Hannah Arendt wrote that "the need of reason is not inspired by the quest for truth but by the quest for meaning." Truth can be verified. Meaning must be constructed — slowly, through sustained reflection on experience. When we lose the conditions for that kind of reflection — when experience is flattened into content, when attention is auctioned to the highest bidder, when every image circulates without interpretation — we don't just lose art. We lose the ability to imagine a future different from the present.

Art is dually a critique of the generation preceding it and an invitation for society to realize a new potential. Every painting that breaks with tradition, every building that reimagines a city, every orchestra that places an instrument in a child's hands — these are acts of imagination that insist the world is not fixed. That it can be otherwise. That the way things are is not the way things must remain.

---

I think about this when I return to the Rothko room at SFMOMA. The painting still says nothing. But I am learning to sit with it longer, to let the silence do its work, to notice what shifts in me when I stop demanding that the painting explain itself. It is a small practice — almost embarrassingly small against the scale of the problems I've described. But I am starting to believe that the willingness to look, to stay, to form a thought that belongs to no one but yourself, is where everything begins.
