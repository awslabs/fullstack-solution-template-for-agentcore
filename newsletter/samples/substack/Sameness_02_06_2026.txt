Maybe by a stroke of luck, I’ve spent this week tucked into bed recovering from a sickness so painful that I could do little but lie still. In the quiet of weekday afternoons, I ruefully watched daylight I would otherwise miss and passed the hours by writing things down. In bed, I finally had the time to give breath to thoughts I’ve kept in my chest for months—ideas loosely catalogued and shoved between journal entries, waiting for a pause long enough to reach conclusion.

This week felt like the appropriate time to take inventory.

It started with the sickness itself. It is an illness that plagues me every year at the same time—so punctual that I would be more surprised not to spend my early February in deep recovery.

In my feverish state, I woke throughout the night slipping between this bed and last year’s and the year before’s. It felt as though my body could not distinguish where I was in time or place. Instead, it moved erratically between each of the February sick days I had spent in the same writhing ache. The room blurred into memory. The memory blurred into present. I was sick in every year all at once.

It made me think about the strange way that life repeats itself—and about the ways we learn, or fail to learn, how to live with patterns that refuse to break. I like to tell myself that I am evolving, reinventing, beginning again. But sometimes it feels like I am circling into myself.

I confess that sameness frightens me.

This feels urgent to me now because I’ve become more serious about painting in the past few months. I’ve been thinking more carefully about process—about what it means to return to an image, to a gesture, to a question without abandoning it too quickly.

Art has never felt peaceful to me. It is revision and sometimes erasure. It is trying to outrun what I have already made because the idea of making the same thing twice feels like failure.

On some days, I feel so afraid of walking into the same room again, of encountering the same type of person and replaying the same conversation with them like a broken record. To wake up in the same sickness, to make the same paintings, to carry the same memory for years—it can feel like being sealed inside something that’s outside of my control. I get déjà vu so often that I’ve learned to shake it off when life plays mean jokes on me. This sickness is one of them.

And yet, the longer I sit with it, the less certain I am that sameness is the villain of my life.

I start to question whether I waltzed into the sameness like a big smart-aleck. Whether I moved too quickly through people and days, already convinced I knew how they will end. Maybe to protect myself from sameness, I’ve walked into a trap of working backwards—clinging to outcomes without the conviction to push my own imagination forward. Somewhere along the way, my blank canvases turned into cages and sameness felt like being stuck.

But in the wake of this year’s sickness, mulling over everything, I want to believe something different. I want to believe that sameness isn’t stuckness, but it’s something else entirely.

Maybe sameness is like architecture.

Sameness can be everywhere. Like how each day the same sun rises and sets. We return to the same streets, faces, and thoughts. The houses do not build from the ground anew each day; the trees do not rearrange overnight. They are always there. So sometimes it’s safer to walk around the neighborhood focusing on the tree stump that’s always stumped or the neighbor’s ugly porch or the smell of debris that won’t escape the corner. But some days, I feel like noticing that the neighbor’s tree started growing lemons or that daylight lasts a few minutes longer or that I recognize this week’s jazz number at the Golden Gate Park.

On the surface, sameness might appear predictable, safe, uninspiring. But maybe beneath it, something subtler is at work: a slow accumulation of difference. Changes so small they could go unnoticed if we never learn how to look differently at them.

The idea of repetition makes me think of Edvard Munch. Although most famous for The Scream, Munch has painted the same scenes of The Kiss, The Madonna, and others across decades—not to reproduce them, but to approach something in-exhaustive. Each version is the same motif and yet slightly altered. Color and temperature shifting, lines trembling differently, anguish and longing in new interpretations. Repetition was not stagnation for Munch but a process of reflection that required duration and constant re-evaluation. Whether it was the inescapable grip of a past lover or a persisting image of loneliness, a handful of vignettes continued to reappear throughout Munch’s career, almost as if he had no choice but to return again and again. Munch’s repetition was not sameness but a refusal to abandon what still unsettled him.

It comes at no surprise that Munch was also a prolific print-maker and spent more of his life commissioning lithographs than paintings. It seems to me that the method of print-making foils Munch’s choice to “repeat” images over and over again. As if, in his form, he subverts recognizable repetition in subject-matter by adjusting his choice of stone, ink, or paper instead. I believe it is inevitable to face recurring themes in one’s life. For an artist like Munch, he was able to capture these recurrences with honesty and perhaps more fearlessness than he’s given credit for. It is incredibly difficult to confront sickness, passion, and loneliness again and again, but for Munch it was his way of life, of art-making.

Another painter I think of, who appears radically different from Munch on the surface, is Giorgio Morandi. He is a painter who painted plain cups and bottles on a small table in Bologna for most of his life. The objects barely change. The arrangement is almost monastic. Yet the air between each frame shifts; the light thickens; the silence becomes animate and personable. Morandi’s repetition of form is dutiful. It seems that Morandi learned how to return to his table every day without the fear of sameness. There is no fever in his repetition. Only patience. Morandi trusts that if he looks long enough, something new will reveal itself within what appears unchanged.

I believe that neither Munch nor Morandi returned to the same images because they were stuck. They learned how to look again without demanding that their subject justify itself through novelty—without clinging to the fantasy or fear that it will remain unchanged. There is a common belief in the sameness that calls them to return, a faith that sameness is just a structure and what’s living within it is fiercely alive.

I talk about painters like Edvard Munch and Giorgio Morandi as if I’ve just stumbled upon them. In truth, I owe more to them than I can articulate. Studying painters and their peculiar lives has taught me things I don’t yet have language for —there are not enough words to encapsulate the impact that artists have on the way I see the world .

I might not know every detail about their lives, but encountering a painting can feel strangely intimate. To see what someone else chose to look at—to notice what held their attention long enough to be painted again and again—feels like hearing things unsaid but so presently alive in the image.

In my own process, I am learning to confront sameness repeatedly.

When I paint, I find that my process leads me towards the same gestures, sometimes with fascination, sometimes with unease. There is a familiar rhythm I find in stretching canvases and building the surface bit by bit. As I move through a painting, my arm grows weary. My hand grips a paintbrush unevenly saturated—some bristles too short, others inconsistently wet or dry. The motion softens. I become bored, then faintly sickened. With patchiness and clumsiness, I speckle the surface with a mosaic of squares that are not exactly squares. No shape shares the same opacity. Each mark arrives as a slight deviation: a succession of different characters, units of time, accidents of fatigue and chance I cannot cheat.

I am imprecise.

And yet there is a lesson in this repetition.

I’ve learned that while sameness is everywhere, if one pauses—really pauses—to observe the layers of moments that compose these “same” days, an unmistakable irreproducibility emerges. If I try to repeat a gesture exactly, it resists me. The body intervenes. Time alters everything. Even within deliberate repetition, variation will insist on itself. No brushstroke lands with the same pressure twice. No conversation repeats without subtle shifts in tone, memory, or desire.

I believe sameness, then, is only an illusion of distance.

Underneath it is variation—fragile, persistent, inspiring.

I find myself optimistic by this outlook. Lying in bed this February, slipping between years, I felt trapped inside recurrence. But Munch and Morandi suggest another possibility: that to return to the same room, the same body, the same ache, might not be imprisonment. Perhaps the exhaustion I felt was never from sameness itself, but my resistance to seeing what was different each time.

By taking a closer look at the sameness in front of me, my patience quells the familiar discomfort and transfixes me instead—it is now, for the first time, I am seeing the details of difference, the fine-lines I didn’t notice, silhouettes I took for granted. Taking a closer look, it seems that nothing was ever the same at all.
