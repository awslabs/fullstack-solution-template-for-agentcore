Lately, I’ve been thinking less about disbelief and more about exhaustion.

Not the thrilling atheism of the 19th century, not even the grand negations of modernism, but a quieter, more ambient faithlessness—diffuse, ironic, almost compulsory. It seems to me like everything has already been dismantled: starting with God, then tradition, representation, authority, even meaning. And yet the dismantling continues long after the structure has collapsed. Our once common center, has exploded into endless simultaneity, of haphazard circles and directionless spiral.

Contemporary art mirrors this condition almost too well. It’s not that art has lost its critical edge; it’s that critique itself has become ambient. Automated. Consequence-free. When everything resists, nothing resists. When everything is simultaneous, nothing compels response. When everything is red, nothing is red. You get the idea.

It’s from this exhaustion that I’ve found myself returning to Gutai—not necessarily as history or style, but as a way of thinking today.

Gutai and the Commitment to Form
Founded in postwar Japan in 1954, the Gutai Art Association did not seek chaos for its own sake. Its violence toward material and rejection of representation were not gestures of nihilism but acts of commitment. As Jirō Yoshihara wrote in The Gutai Manifesto (1956), Gutai art aimed “to unite the material he had chosen with his own spiritual dynamics.” The artist did not dominate matter, nor erase it into concept, but served as a vessel through which material could become spiritual.

“Gutai art does not change the material but brings it to life,” Yoshihara insisted. This was not metaphor. It was ontology.

Kazuo Shiraga’s performances—painting suspended from a rope, feet plunging into pigment—were not theatrical provocations meant for an audience. He had no interest in spectacle. This was simply the method that allowed him to commit the whole self with the body. The body was not a signifier but the site of belief.

Likewise, each member of Gutai sought to create works beyond the abstract expression of their predecessors. While Shiraga moved his body through material, Atsuko Tanaka dressed-up in lightbulbs and Shozo Shimamoto blew up cans of paint. Practicing Gutai was never about one intuitive method; it was about constant and playful expansion. Once a piece had emerged, the next required radical adjustment, not to mimic but to evolve.

Ultimately Gutai’s rejection of representation was not a rejection of meaning, but of imitation. Yoshihara warned against the “counterfeit objects of the altars” or (in some translations) the “heaps of simulacra” that merely replicate past reality without new risks. What mattered was not novelty in itself, but the outward expansion of form through material encounter rather than simplification into symbolism.

This distinction matters now more than ever.

After Representation
Susan Sontag once described interpretation as a form of violence. To interpret, she argued, was to turn art into an article for use, something to be arranged into neat mental categories. What she wanted instead was a shift in attention—from content to presence, from meaning to form. “In place of a hermeneutics,” she wrote, “we need an erotics of art.”

She was writing at a moment when representation still held enormous power in the art world. Arguably today, the problem has inverted itself. Representation hasn’t just been dismantled; it’s been replaced by a culture of frictionless experience, where sensation circulates without interpretation and presence without consequence. Erotics, once a corrective, has hardened into another form of consumption.

What Gutai understood—and what contemporary art increasingly forgets—is that form without discipline collapses into spectacle. At scale, this stream of formless art creates a society of reaction, but too dumb to articulate real responses. Contemporary art has effectively eliminated the need for visual language, and in turn, seized our ability to name our sensations. When the art loses grammar, the viewer struggles to see anything at all. Most will walk away unconcernedly with nods of oohs and aahs.

Sontag might never have expected “content” to take on this new meaning. Content is no longer a conclusion but the impenetrable absence of diction; it is a body without ritual or focus. This is the paradox of the present moment: in our rush to escape meaning through interpretation, we’ve lost the capacity to believe in form as its replacement. We are stuck instead in a vicious cycle of disbelief and consumption, where we permit formless slop for the sake of a spectacle.

Thinking, Time, Responsibility
I keep returning to a line from Hannah Arendt that has been sitting with me for months: that our need for reason is driven less by the search for truth than by the search for meaning. Meaning, for Arendt, isn’t something proven or verified; it’s something generated slowly, through sustained reflection on experience.

This is why Arendt is so skeptical of modern faith in cognition or scientific thinking alone. Thinking, as she understands it, isn’t intelligence or technical mastery. It’s the repetitive, often uncomfortable work of remembering, judging, and reckoning with what one has done and witnessed. It doesn’t aim at mastery over the world, but at reconciliation with it.

She insists, too, that thinking is inseparable from appearance—from the fact that we live among others, on a shared stage, answerable to what we say and do. We are not merely in the world; we are of it. Responsibility is not an add-on to thinking. It’s built into it (cognito, ergo sum).

What threatens this capacity today isn’t disbelief in God so much as the erosion of the conditions that make thinking possible at all. When experience is flattened into content, memory outsourced to algorithms, and judgment replaced by optimization, the basic structure of thought collapses.

The result isn’t freedom. It’s thoughtlessness. It is stupidity.

This collapse, Arendt suggests, offers a strange opportunity: to inherit the past “without a will-and-testament” (Notre héritage n’est précédé d’aucun testament), unburdened by prescriptions, yet surrounded by an overwhelming abundance of raw experience. The problem is that abundance without structure does not yield greater access; it yields paralysis.

“The need of reason,” Arendt writes, “is not inspired by the quest for truth but by the quest for meaning. And truth and meaning are not the same.” When meaning dissolves, reason does not disappear—it turns technocratic. Science ceases to inquire and begins to govern. Optimization, surveillance, efficiency: these are not the fruits of science as wonder, but of science as authority. We killed God with science, perhaps—but now we no longer believe in science either.

Catechism Without God
Perhaps there is an increasing restlessness we all feel about letting art lose their traditional coherence. We have sat uncomfortably idle, pushing down our urge to represent and to interpret. This weekend, I read the most recent essay by Jacques J. Rancourt that describes the poetry of Larry Levis in a refreshing way. He writes that Levis was governed by a “catechistic process” of memory, confession, and penance. Rancourt notes that Levis doesn’t invoke religious symbolism so much as practice a form of temporal discipline, a locality of truth.

Memory, in Levis’s work, is not nostalgia but refusal—the refusal to let experience disappear. His confession is not a performance but a private responsibility to acknowledge the weight of time and consequence. Both of these are necessary for his story-like poems to find conclusion in the trauma of loss, exile, or politics.

Together, these form a rhythm opposed to the logic of the times we live in. Catechism demands that nothing is instantaneous, frictionless, or without cost. It runs counter to the algorithmic promise of endless revision without accountability or effort. In laying out the rules, we negate the idea of production without remembrance, or of experience without intention. Since science cannot provide structures for thinking beyond what is true, catechism offers us a way to investigate and meditate on what is meaningful. Our thinking must live in a body with limits and morals to guide us, one that agrees with itself by answering to a foundation of catechistic ritual and purpose.

Why This Matters for Art
Art, like thinking, requires duration. It requires a body capable of remembering itself across time. This is what Gutai understood instinctively. Its insistence on bodily commitment, material resistance, and irreversibility placed the artist inside a catechistic structure—one in which action could not be undone, outsourced, or abstracted. One could not “cheat” time.

Without memory, confession, or penance, Larry Levis might still have written about Caravaggio with care but without reckoning: the sense that time has passed, that new choices have been made, that something is owed to what came before. Instead, Levis’s memory of Goliath confronts the death of his friend in Vietnam and the sense of loss beyond just what happened. His structure demands recount and force in a true struggle. The resistance is no longer aestheticized or tautological. It peers into Levis’s very soul.

Contemporary art, by contrast, increasingly mirrors the fickle ethos of technology: instant legibility, endless circulation, and possession. Repetition in art is becoming less about meditation and more about commodification. In the world of concept art, an artist must create something instantly recognizable and economically sustainable. The goal is to make manufactured art and pretend it isn’t. We are no longer responding and innovating to the traditions before us, we are killing chronology altogether. The sell is the system, not the ritual, not the body.

Catechism offers one name for the kind of fidelity art demands: not belief, but commitment. Rather than invent a neue-doctrine or manifesto, we simply must accept the basic responsibility that’s required to construct a place for deeper meaning.

God Is Dead, but Form Is Not
Whether Hegel or Nietzsche first declared that God is dead, it’s undeniable that disbelief is no longer a radical position—it’s the default. The more interesting question is what collapsed in His absence. What disappeared alongside theology were the shared structures that once allowed meaning to endure.

Perhaps we mistook repetition for stagnation and discipline for oppression. In doing so, we lost not only belief, but the strength required to return—to do something again and again without irony or exhaustion, to obey form because it provides liberation not authority.

G.K. Chesterton names this sentiment so clearly. Children, he writes, want repetition because they have the vitality to say: “Do it again.”

Perhaps God, too, has the strength for that kind of repetition, and says every day: “Do it again” to the sun and the moon and the stars. In this sense, God is not old but endlessly young—possessed of “the eternal appetite of infancy.”

Somewhere between every morning and night—the unwavering physics that governs everything—there is an infinity of life to witness in the details. As Yoshihara says, “Gutai Art is the product that has arisen from the pursuit of possibilities.” To think like Gutai is to practice a certain humility: to see that each day possesses possibility for “new matter” and new spirit. Within units of physicality, time, and material, Gutai art emerges from the structures that embody spiritual meaning. Gutai does not escape form; it inhabits it, allowing form to nurture meaning rather than erase it.

Form is ultimately what allows our intention to last long enough to take a life of their own. Without form, art dissolves into meaningless gesture and accidental noise—a chaos of thoughtlessness rather than freedom. Form does not merely impose belief or offer a defense for art; it makes art possible. God may be dead, but form outlives us all.
