Before moving to the city, I remember being more angry.

My journal entries from three months ago use words like “tragedies” and “rage” across pages of turbulent thinking. I wrote flurried grievances about the world we live in, and I let my sentences run on into stretches of inconclusive outcry. I allowed myself the space to witness the horrific headlines with rawness and sensitivity. Just a while ago, I felt differently about everything—as somehow more real and more true, more consequential and affecting.

But since moving to the city, my words have quietly disappeared into the corners of my mind. Every day, there is the greater stimuli of traffic and danger to occupy my attention. The emotions I carried with me here are swallowed and outsized in every way. Despite the abundance of events that happens each day in a city like this, I feel as if I witness nothing at all.

Where I once romanticized the raw sort of living, I now feel quite the opposite. I take a bus through downtown, where the streets are lined with banks and homeless people. I watch people fight on the streets and on the bus, yelling slurs and acting crazily, all of which go ignored by the bus riders, too busy on their phones. I see so much but feel less than what makes sense. Maybe this is the rawness that’s only glamorous in the movies—slummy, exciting chaos that lives in the distance. I don’t mean it as a complaint, but as an acknowledgment that there is a certain condition I must confront to live here and feel sane.

As my life begins to take hold, the city has infected or perhaps medicated me into a state of anomie, tolerance, survival. The strange membrane between me, us, them, confuses my understanding of place. Where bodies might collide and share seats, we are never really together. Like Debord might say, we are a “looking” society, a group of spectators that stare at each other like untouchable surfaces.

It seems that here, in the city, life intervenes more sovereignly than us city mortals. The signal light chimes, the wallet empties, the crowd moves on. “It was inevitable,” we say. Just like the inevitable commuters who meander without soul or the inevitable man yelling obscenities my way. The bus that inevitably takes me home to sleep off the heartache I felt today. That feeling of inevitability holds my anger long enough till I forget what happened.

To feel fully is more impossible in a place like this. How can I confront a never-ending queue of troubled people, discarded by society but sitting right there in front of me. I feel useless and appalling to myself—like a bystander to the injustice that happens silently each day. There is this dissonant reality that is in many ways perpetuated by people like me: people who stand up to the headlines because it’s easier than making conversation with the difficult man on the bus. It feels easy to commiserate about the news in the living room, but too radical to practice generosity outside.

“It was inevitable,” I might say again.

But inevitability is not a fact—it is just a feeling.

The term for this sensation is called “hypernormalisation”—the feeling that the system is unjust, absurd, and yet immovable. In the BBC documentary HyperNormalisation (2016), Adam Curtis describes a world in which politicians, financiers, and citizens accept that the structures governing them no longer make sense. Rather than confront the complexity or risk dismantling it, everyone agrees, tacitly, to behave as if the system is coherent. A fake world is constructed because the real one feels too overwhelming to repair. We don’t believe that everything is fine but we pretend like it is inevitable.

Hypernormalisation is not a delusion but a subconscious survival instinct. It is the learned habit of navigating a broken system without demanding that it change, continuing to scroll and to swipe while knowing that reality is shaky. Curtis suggests that the great political failure of our time is not apathy but imagination. We struggle to picture alternatives to what currently exists, choosing to tolerate our circumstances rather than confront the gravity of its issues. And so the present, however dysfunctional, hardens into our “inevitable” destiny.

The city’s overstimulation does not merely exhaust the senses. German sociologist Georg Simmel wrote in "The Metropolis and Mental Life" (1903) that the city forces a psychic defense—a dulling of response to everything—because no nervous system can endure constant intensity. In the constant flood of images, advertisements, crises, and strangers, our experience becomes flattened into Debordian spectacle. And once life is lived at the level of spectacle, its contradictions lose urgency.

Simmel also argues that cities tend to stagnate because the burden of its problems become diluted. In a city, responsibility disperses across millions of people until it becomes weightless and unimportant. Economists call this a collective action problem; psychologists call it diffusion of responsibility. Whatever the name, the effect is the same: everyone sees the issue, and everyone quietly assumes someone else will fix it. We point fingers, we get mad, we stand far enough from others to blame them and say, “It's not us, it's the bank. The bank isn't like a man. Or an owner with 50,000 acres, he isn't like a man either. That's the monster....”

The philosopher Hannah Arendt once described a similar condition, which she called the “banality of evil.” Observing the trial of Nazi criminal Adolf Eichmann, she expected to find a monstrous figure orchestrating cruelty. Instead she found a bureaucrat—ordinary, obedient, frighteningly unremarkable. The danger, she concluded, was not monstrous hatred but thoughtlessness: the tendency for individuals to participate in systems without reflecting on the consequences of their actions. Modern systems do not require villains to function; they require routine and, in Eichmann’s case, obedience. In a city, routines appear everywhere—in the commute, in the scrolling screen, in the quiet decision to look away. The system reproduces itself not because people are evil, but because they stop thinking about what they are doing.

“The sad truth is that most evil is done by people who never make up their minds to be either good or evil.” — Hannah Arendt, The Life of the Mind (1978)

Whatever feels socially automatic is probably a form of dysfunction. Cities, at scale, do not naturally reward kindness or selflessness. Rather, we are taught implicitly to become resourceful, smart, and independent; or more accurately, to become greedy, cunning, and selfish. Our instincts tell us something is wrong. But when we place ourselves in the city—inside its constant movement and detachment—those instincts become difficult to interpret. We start to protect our zone of interest and let the rest of our mind run in auto-pilot, a sort of liminal space of moral indecision. If we suspend our intentions long enough, we are never culpable for walking past injustices. If we never commit to doing good, there is a no fear of disappointment or failure. We remain harmless.

In no world should I feel compelled to see a part of Adolf Eichmann in myself or in anyone. And yet, I can’t help but recognize this stupefying state of paralysis, of bystanding, of aimless and thoughtless cruelty I may be capable of to protect myself from the mess that’s out there. I worry that I shut down and blindly trust a fallible expectation of basic decency in myself. I worry that I believe people are inherently good; that it would be impossible to completely lose a sense of empathy.

And so, for a long time I thought the problem was simply that the world had fallen asleep. Unlike the hardened politicians and the corrupt businessmen, I used to believe that my anger was proof that I was not asleep. My outrage convinced me that I was a different type of person, or perhaps a better type of person, than the others. Now I am not so sure.

Hollywood movies like The Matrix (1999) suggest that awakening is decisive: one moment you swallow the red pill and the illusion collapses. Reality reveals itself, terrifying, but finally coherent. The hero steps up, and the system begins to crack.

The reality is that life rarely grants moments like that. Most of us are not heroes prepared to shatter the world we inhabit, most of us do not even care. I had always believed that I would do the right thing once faced with doomsday, and yet I am starting to realize that I am not Neo in this script, not even close. I don’t feel “chosen” for the job of fixing society’s problems so I let myself sit in the background and wait for someone better to come along.

This train of thought is probably the trap that every person in the world has fallen into. The evil politicians and businessmen who ruin the city tell themselves a version of “inevitability” like the rest of us do. The scale is larger and the harms are greater, but we are all still human at the end of the day. Perhaps these executives get angry too. They probably yell and moan about all sorts of problems far removed from the rest of us.

Living in the intimacy of a city, I start to realize that we are all in the same haze. Not fully asleep, but too tired to care. The strong ones learn how to climb out of this moral pitfall, but most never do.

I asked myself for months, how I might break away from this weariness. I devised a lesson, a ritual, a prayer to keep my senses alight. Nothing really worked. I sat at my desk, journal gaping open, pen in hand, and not a thought in mind. I frustrated myself at my lack of capacity. I confessed to myself all the hatred I had for this city. I just wanted to soothe myself until the world felt less scary.

But the problem with these ineffective, self-calming exercises was the same root problem of indecision. I was devising a tactic for coping without committing to a moral standard I might fail at. I started to realize that my anger was just a way of reacting to the world without taking responsibility for it. A bigger, angrier reaction doesn’t initiate bigger change or any change at all. The only hope is the fearsome commitment to take responsibility in the ways we know how—to choose decisive action over our reactions and become stronger at it each day. Without commitment, journaling about my conflicted and angry feelings would never amount to anything. I had to decide who I wanted to be and stick with it; even if it meant falling short or feeling like a bad person.

Today, I am less emotional about the world and more interested in defining the rules I live by. It’s terrifying to commit to a moral impetus because it means my mistakes become concrete. But the alternative is even worse. I fear becoming an indecisive, thoughtless person, who believes that their inherent goodness is default off of no basis, no discipline, or evidence beyond the measure of peacetime. Today, I recognize that I am in a season that demands me to be clear about the person I want to become. I am growing confidence in my internal rulebook of personal outlines, protocols and pursuits. I am becoming more imaginative about the world I want to live in and the world I want to become. Committing is scary, but it involves me in the future I’m setting out to live more voraciously than “inevitability” could ever permit.

One day, I might not live in a city and I might not remember all the things I am feeling. I will probably only remember the good parts and reminisce about the skyline or the park or the local grocer. All that’s to say, this moment in my life is a unique opportunity to live and to test my theories intimately. Now I believe that there is no better place to become a person—to protect the choices I make to care and to step into the standard of life I want to live.

If you made it this far, I hope you are somewhere contemplating your personhood because it truly is your own. I hope you are someone who is making your mind about the life you want to lead and taking ownership over your successes and your failures. I am cheering you and me (us) in our collective pursuit to imagine and shape a happier world.
