Poetry is barbarism

"Nach Auschwitz ein Gedicht zu schreiben, ist barbarisch."

“To write poetry after Auschwitz is barbaric,” said Adorno. 

It was the 1951 Adorno who said, or perhaps decreed, this provocative dictum against art—a statement that has been interpreted, misinterpreted, and deconstructed in a thousand essays, cited as both a prohibition and an imperative of art-making in the 20th century.

For German philosopher Theodore Adorno, there could be no aesthetic theorie, no politics or prose to dutifully represent the horrors of the Holocaust. To him, the art forms that had flourished in Europe, celebrated for centuries as expressions of human achievement, had proven irrelevant in the face of Auschwitz, Treblinka, and Dachau. Could an elegy, a painting, or a sonnet ever hope to encapsulate such suffering? To Adorno, this could only be barbarism. 

And yet, the poets, the artists, filmmakers, and writers continued to create art after Auschwitz, recognizing the paradox that art was both necessary and impossible in the face of its horrors. Filmmaker Jean-Luc Godard once said that traces of cinema are legacies of an assassination barely felt—the fleeting evidence of something exterminated, inflicted and forgotten. 

In this, Godard says, “Poets are mortals who, singing earnestly, sense the trace of the fugitive gods, stay in these tracks, and trace for the mortals, their brothers.” In other words, poets are the mortals who must remember what is lost.

The death of Alan Kurdi

Among the hundred thousands killed in the Syrian refugee crisis, we will remember the name of a 2-year old boy who drowned off the coast of Turkey. His name was Alan Kurdi. 

The image of his small body, face-down on the Mediterranean shoreline, garnered attention around the world and became an emblem of innocence lost in conflict. And yet, Alan’s death was shocking not because it was unique but because it was captured. The Western world, largely indifferent to the plight of Syrian refugees up to that point, found itself staring at an undeniable truth: the refugee crisis was not an abstract issue. It had a face. It had a name.

But this moment of reckoning also raised ethical questions. The photograph of Alan’s body was splashed across newspaper covers, social media feeds, and television screens. Was this a form of necessary witness or an act of voyeurism? The line became blurry.

In the aftermath, people debated whether sharing the image did more harm than good. Charif Kiwan, a founding member of the anonymous Syrian video collective Abounaddara, has long wrestled with this question. “When you focus on death, you say, ‘It’s done. It’s finished,’” Kiwan said in an interview. “There is nothing to do except voyeurism. So here is the problem for us. We have to find a way to represent horror, to represent death, respecting the golden rule—dignity.”

The death of Alan Kurdi confronts the very dilemma that Adorno posed decades earlier: the complex relationship between the barbarism and the need for representation. The painful insufficiency of art to address atrocity, and yet its undeniable power to remember—to witness what ought to never be witnessed.

The image of Alan Kurdi did not solve the refugee crisis. It did not end the war in Syria. But it did compel the world to pause, to confront, if only for a moment, its own inhumanity—its own barbarism. 

The New Adorno

By the time Adorno revisited his earlier statement in 1970, his thinking had deepened. The idea that poetry—or any art—was barbaric after Auschwitz had come to seem too simplistic, too absolute for what came after. The horrors of the 20th century had not ended with the Holocaust. Soon, the Vietnam War, the revolutions, and the violence of colonialism all demanded to be remembered. If art could not speak to these atrocities, then what could? Silence, it seemed, was no longer an option.

To this, the 1970 Adorno professed that “Without historical remembrance, there would be no beauty.” In other words, without memory—without the act of bearing witness—beauty itself would be hollow. 

To move forward, Adorno argued, art needed to confront the past, not with prettified abstractions, but with a sober engagement with memory. The world had changed, and art had to change with it. Adorno saw that old ways of creating, of beautifying, no longer had a place in this new reality. He was no longer calling for a cessation of art, but for a reimagining of its purpose.

And this is where Adorno’s journey leads us—not to the rejection of art, but to its transformation. If the old forms of representation were insufficient, then new forms must be created. 

Art is memory

Elie Wiesel, Holocaust survivor and Nobel laureate, once wrote, “For the dead and the living, we must bear witness.” To forget was to allow the dead to die a second time. But to remember, to create art in the aftermath of horror, was to take on a profound responsibility.

Wiesel’s words echo the path that Adorno ultimately followed. Yes, art is insufficient; this cannot be fixed. But without it—without poetry, without cinema, without painting and music—we lose something essential. We lose the ability to remember. And without memory, there can be no justice.

Godard grappled with this challenge in his magnum opus, Histoire(s) du cinéma—a meditation on the failure of cinema to adequately capture the traumas of the 20th century. For Godard, as for Adorno, traditional modes of representation had failed. But rather than abandon art, Godard sought to create something new: a fragmented, collage-like work that embraced the messiness of memory and the impossibility of full representation. 

His contemporaries, like François Truffaut, Aleksandr Solzhenitsyn, and Günter Grass, addressed this same tension—the limitations and power of art-making in the wake of historical trauma. Solzhenitsyn’s The Gulag Archipelago aimed to memorialize the suffering of millions under the Soviet regime, not by dramatizing their experience but by meticulously documenting their daily realities. Grass, too, in The Tin Drum, sought to grapple with the weight of Germany’s past, threading the grotesque absurdities of war into his characters and narrative, offering no neat answers but demanding that his readers remember.

In contemporary cinema, filmmaker Kirsten Johnson tackles similar issues in her documentary Cameraperson (2016), which, like Godard, collages footage from her 25-year career as a cinematographer. In one scene, she befriends a young Afghan boy whose brother was killed by a bomb strike in the Afghan War. In another, she captures the aftermath of conflict in postwar Bosnia, where the ghosts of violence linger in everyday life. Johnson’s work, like Godard's, challenges the ethics of observation and the responsibilities of an artist in confronting horror. For artists like Johnson, the camera becomes a tool for capturing the unbearable and yet unavoidable truth of human suffering—art that offers memory as a form of resistance.

We must

The relationship between art and horror will always be fraught. There is no simple solution, no perfect way to represent the grotesque realities of war, genocide, and suffering. But that does not absolve us of the responsibility to try.

In this, Adorno’s journey becomes ours in our daily interactions with reality’s horrific truths, traumas, and conflicts—all of which will demand our remembrance again and again. Art may be barbaric after Auschwitz, after Alan Kurdi, after every unspeakable atrocity. But it is also necessary. It is through art that we remember, that we bear witness, that we keep the memory of the dead alive. 

And perhaps, in the act of remembrance, there is a beauty that transcends the barbarism of the world. Because to write poetry after Auschwitz is barbaric, but we must.
