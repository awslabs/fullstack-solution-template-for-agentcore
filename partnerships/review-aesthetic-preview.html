Memory and Matter · Floor 6

There's a room on the sixth floor where the air feels different. Kiefer's Mann im Wald hangs at a scale that makes you forget you're in a museum — it feels more like standing at the edge of a forest that burned down decades ago and never quite recovered.

The curation here is exceptional. Rather than treating Kiefer and Kentridge as parallel figures, the rooms create a conversation between them — German postwar guilt on one side, South African apartheid on the other, connected by the shared language of ash, charcoal, and erasure.

Kiefer room, Floor 6

I sat in the listening area for twenty minutes. The Bay Area poets reading responses to the work — that was the detail that elevated this from a great show to something I'll remember for years. Go on a weekday morning if you can.

Calder, Kelly, LeWitt · Floor 5

The Kelly room stopped me cold. There's a shaped canvas — just two colors, a curve — that somehow holds more tension than paintings ten times its complexity. The Fishers clearly understood something about collecting that most people miss: depth over breadth.

"The Fishers clearly understood something about collecting that most people miss: depth over breadth."
Ellsworth Kelly gallery, Floor 5

Calder's mobiles are installed with enough space to actually breathe — you can watch them move. The LeWitt wall drawings feel almost architectural at this scale. Three artists, one floor, zero filler.

Femme au chapeau · Floor 4

The recreation of the 1905 Salon room is a clever conceit, but I left wanting more. The wall text leans heavily on provenance and biography — the Steins, the Haas bequest — and less on what actually makes this painting radical. If you already know the Fauvist context, the show doesn't push you further.

That said, seeing the original alongside works by Derain and Vlaminck in something approximating their original arrangement is genuinely rare. Worth the visit for that alone.
